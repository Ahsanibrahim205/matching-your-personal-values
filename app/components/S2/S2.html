What are personal
values?

  • The ways in which we see our world are influenced by the beliefs and values that shape our lives and give us purpose. Values define what is important to us. Each person has a unique set of values.
  • Values determine the criteria by which we judge whether something is worthwhile or not. Thus, if we value healthy eating, we may reject activities that do not support this value.
  • Values set the filters which direct attention to some things rather than others. Values also motivate. They influence what we move towards or away from.
  • Conflicts may occur between values. For instance, some values may be mutually exclusive – like those influencing the desire to be slender and those leading us towards cream cakes. We may then waver from one value to the other – and shift in our behaviour from overeating to crash dieting. On the other hand, some values may consistently override others – for a workaholic, work may win out over play. All values are useful, but some are more useful than others.
  • Usually we know some of our values at the conscious level, recognising perhaps that ‘Health’ and ‘Wellness’ are important to us. Other values may be unknown to us, but yet influence us. Both the values we are aware of and those we don’t know can influence us unconsciously. When we find ourselves feeling uncomfortable or rejecting something because it ‘feels wrong’, it is generally because some value is active. The value may not necessarily be in our conscious awareness, but nonetheless influences the quality and direction of our behaviour.
  • Becoming more aware of our values and how they relate to each other helps us become more aware of what is motivating us and allows us greater choice. As we discover what’s truly important to us, our life’s direction naturally comes into alignment with that.
  • Our values strongly affect whether we are congruent about a goal we have set ourselves. They represent what is important to us and are supported by beliefs. Values are related to our identity. We really care about them; they are the fundamental principles we live by. To act against our values will make us incongruent. Values give us motivation and direction. The most lasting and influential values are freely chosen and not imposed. They are chosen with awareness of the consequences, and carry many positive feelings.

    • Here are some examples of value statements associated with the work, social / recreational and family environments.

  • You may state that you think it is important that your work involvement is most satisfying when it
    1. Is team based
    2. Offers opportunities for taking initiative
    3. Is a balanced part of life with fairly tight boundaries
    4. Offers you a chance to develop yourself as a person
  • That your social / recreational involvement is most satisfying when it offers some
    1. Elements of challenge
    2. Provides opportunities to learn something new on a regular basis
    3. Allows you to put something good back into society
    4. Provides time to unwind and relax.
  • That your family life is best when
    1. It allows time together as a whole family
    2. Family members engage in some recreational/ social activities together
    3. The family has reasonable financial security
    4. Routine tasks and chores are efficiently dealt with on a basis of mutual co-operation
  • In sum, what is important to you – your personal values - is a life that has every chance of being satisfying and leading to a higher sense of personal well-being.
  • When satisfaction is low, the way forward is firstly to become aware of what your values are and then to examine how you are currently trying to realise them through your activities across each of your life spaces.
